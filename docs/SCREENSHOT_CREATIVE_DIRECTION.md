# Checkpoint — Screenshot & Visual Creative Direction

## The Approach: Brutalist Documentary

The screenshots should feel like they were shot by a photographer, not generated by a mockup tool. Real environments, real light, real surfaces — but composed with the same restraint and intentionality as the app itself.

Think: **product photography meets automotive documentary**. The phone is an object in a real space. The space tells you something about the person who uses this app. But nothing is styled within an inch of its life — no artisan coffee, no succulents, no perfectly weathered leather journal. This is a utility for people who actually maintain their cars.

### The Rules

1. **Real environments only.** Garage workbench, driver's seat, hood of a car, kitchen counter with a set of car keys. Places where someone would actually check their maintenance schedule.
2. **Natural or hard light.** No diffused studio softboxes. Garage fluorescents, sunlight through a windshield, a single work lamp. If the light is a little harsh, that's honest.
3. **Shallow depth of field.** Your mirrorless camera's biggest advantage. The phone screen stays tack sharp; the environment falls off into bokeh. This keeps the UI legible while the background provides atmosphere.
4. **Desaturated environments, vivid screen.** Grade the real-world photography slightly desaturated and cool. The phone screen — with its cerulean blue and cream — becomes the warmest, most saturated thing in frame. The app *glows* in context.
5. **Zero styling props.** No decorative objects. If something is in frame, it has a reason: car keys, a wrench, a receipt, an owner's manual, a VIN sticker. Functional objects only.
6. **Monospace captions overlay.** Each screenshot gets a caption in JetBrains Mono (or SF Mono), set in cream (#F5F0DC) on a dark bar or directly over the desaturated background. Uppercase, tracked out, minimal. The caption is architecture, not decoration.

## The Six Shots

### Shot 1: "KNOW WHAT'S NEXT"
**The hero shot. This sells the app.**

**Scene:** Phone lying face-up on a dark garage workbench or tool chest. A few functional items nearby — a shop rag, a socket wrench, maybe the edge of a parts receipt. The phone screen shows the Home tab with the "Next Up" card highlighting an overdue oil change in red.

**Camera:** Overhead or ~60° angle. f/2.8 or wider so the tools go soft. The screen is the sharpest thing in frame.

**Light:** Overhead garage fluorescent or a warm work lamp from the side, casting a directional shadow off the phone.

**Composite:** Photograph the phone (screen can be off or showing a solid color for easier replacement). Composite the actual app screenshot onto the screen in post. Match perspective and add a subtle screen reflection/glass sheen for realism.

**Caption placement:** Top of frame, above the phone. `KNOW WHAT'S NEXT` in cream on the dark background.

**Subcaption:** `Your most urgent service. One glance.`

---

### Shot 2: "DATE AND MILEAGE"
**The differentiator. No competitor markets this.**

**Scene:** Phone resting on an open owner's manual or service booklet. The manual is slightly out of focus underneath. Maybe a pen nearby. The screen shows the service detail view with both due-date and due-mileage axes visible.

**Camera:** ~45° angle, slightly closer crop than Shot 1. The owner's manual provides context without competing with the screen.

**Light:** Warm indoor light — kitchen table or desk lamp. The manual's pages catch some warm light.

**Composite:** Same technique. App screenshot composited onto the phone screen.

**Caption:** `DATE AND MILEAGE` at the top. `Whichever comes first.` as subcaption.

---

### Shot 3: "SCAN. LOG. DONE."
**The "wow, that's easy" moment. Camera OCR in action.**

**Scene:** Phone held in hand (your hand, from your perspective — first-person POV), pointed at a real service receipt or an actual odometer/dashboard. The receipt or dashboard is visible but blurred in the background. The phone screen shows the camera OCR view or the auto-filled service form.

**Camera:** First-person perspective (over-the-shoulder or POV). Shallow DOF — the phone screen is sharp, the receipt/odometer behind it is bokeh'd.

**Important Apple note:** Apple's guidelines say you can't show "images of people holding devices" — but a first-person hand holding a phone (where you can't see the person, just the hand/phone/environment) exists in a gray area. **Safer alternative:** Phone propped up or lying on a surface next to the receipt, with the camera having just scanned it. The auto-filled form is on screen, the receipt is next to the phone. Both visible, phone sharp, receipt slightly soft.

**Composite:** App screenshot composited. If showing the camera view, the background behind the phone should show the receipt naturally.

**Caption:** `SCAN. LOG. DONE.` — the period-separated cadence implies speed.

---

### Shot 4: "SEE WHAT YOU SPEND"
**Costs and analytics — the practical payoff.**

**Scene:** Phone on a clean, dark surface — maybe a kitchen counter or a desk. Next to the phone: car keys and nothing else. The screen shows the Costs tab with the spending chart, cost-per-mile, and category breakdown. The composition is clean and minimal.

**Camera:** Overhead flat-lay. The keys add one human touch. The rest is negative space.

**Light:** Even, slightly cool ambient light. Let the phone screen be the color center.

**Composite:** App screenshot composited.

**Caption:** `SEE WHAT YOU SPEND` — straightforward, no cleverness needed.

**Subcaption:** `By category, by month, by mile.`

---

### Shot 5: "EVERYWHERE YOU LOOK"
**The ecosystem shot. This is where you flex the Apple integration.**

**Scene:** This one breaks the single-phone format. Arrange three surfaces of the Apple ecosystem:
- iPhone on a surface showing a Home Screen widget
- Apple Watch on wrist or lying next to the phone showing a complication
- (Optional) iPad or a second phone showing the Lock Screen widget

**Camera:** Wider composition, ~45° angle. All devices should be in the scene but the iPhone is the focal point (sharpest). The Watch can be slightly soft.

**Alternative (simpler):** Just the iPhone on a car dashboard mount or propped on the steering wheel, showing the widget. Through the windshield, you see a slightly blurred parking lot or driveway. This says "your car, your phone, always informed" without needing multiple devices.

**Composite:** App screenshots composited onto each device screen.

**Caption:** `EVERYWHERE YOU LOOK`

**Subcaption:** `Home Screen. Lock Screen. Watch. CarPlay.`

---

### Shot 6: "BUILT WITH INTENTION"
**The design statement. Themes as craftsmanship proof.**

**Scene:** This is the one screenshot that *can* be more graphic/composed. Two approaches:

**Option A (Photographic):** The phone lying on a surface that transitions or contrasts — half on a dark surface, half on a light one. The screen shows a split-theme view or a single theme. The composition itself embodies the intentionality.

**Option B (Graphic):** Break from photography for this one. Use a pure cerulean blue background with 4–6 phone screens arranged in a grid, each showing a different theme. This is the "palette" shot. It's the most "App Store conventional" screenshot, but earned — you've built credibility with the photography in shots 1–5, so this clean graphic closer feels like a deliberate choice, not laziness.

**Recommendation:** Option B. Five shots of photography establish the premium, real-world tone. The sixth shifts to a clean graphic that showcases the theme range. The contrast makes both stronger.

**Caption:** `BUILT WITH INTENTION`

**Subcaption:** `8 themes. Zero fluff.`

## Photography Shot List

### What You Need to Photograph

| Shot | Environment | Key Props | Phone Screen State |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Garage / workbench / tool area | Wrench, shop rag, receipt edge | Solid dark color (for compositing) |
| 2 | Table / desk / counter | Owner's manual or service booklet, pen | Solid dark color |
| 3 | Next to a receipt or near a dashboard | Service receipt (real or printed) | Solid dark color |
| 4 | Clean dark surface | Car keys only | Solid dark color |
| 5 | Car interior / dashboard / steering wheel area | The car itself | Solid dark color |
| 6 | N/A (graphic composite) | N/A | N/A |

### Camera Settings Guide

- **Lens:** 35mm or 50mm equivalent. Avoid ultra-wide (distortion looks unserious). A 50mm f/1.8 would be ideal.
- **Aperture:** f/1.8–f/2.8 for the shallow DOF look. Stop down to f/4 only if you need more of the phone in focus.
- **ISO:** As low as you can manage. Garages are dark — a tripod helps.
- **White balance:** Slightly cool (5000–5500K) for the environment. The phone screen composite will add warmth.
- **Format:** RAW. You'll want the latitude for the desaturated-environment grade.
- **Tip:** Put the phone screen on a solid dark blue (#0033BE) background or just turn it off. A dark, uniform screen is easiest to mask and replace in post.

### Post-Production Workflow

1. **Grade the environment:** Desaturate 20–30%, shift slightly cool, lift the blacks slightly (not crushed — more like a matte film look).
2. **Mask the phone screen:** Use the screen edges to create a precise mask. Perspective-warp the app screenshot to match the phone's angle.
3. **Add screen realism:** Subtle glass reflection (a very light gradient or highlight on one edge), maybe 5–10% reduced contrast on the screenshot to simulate viewing through glass. Don't overdo this — the screen should still be the crispest element.
4. **Add caption overlay:** JetBrains Mono, cream (#F5F0DC), tracked uppercase. Position at top of frame with comfortable margin. If the background is too busy at the top, add a subtle dark gradient (not a hard bar — let it fade).
5. **Final dimensions:** 1290 × 2796 px (iPhone 15 Pro Max / 6.9"). This is the primary submission size; Apple auto-scales.

## Mood References

These aren't direct references to copy — they're tone/feeling targets:

- **Halide's marketing:** Photography-heritage aesthetic, reverence for the camera as an object
- **Leica product photography:** Product on raw concrete, single hard light source, minimal props
- **Dieter Rams "less but better":** Every element earns its place
- **Workshop/garage photography:** The beauty of functional spaces — pegboards, tool drawers, grease-stained surfaces

The thread connecting all of these: **objects shown with respect in their natural habitat, lit honestly, composed with intention.**

## What This Achieves in the App Store

When someone scrolls past Checkpoint's listing, they won't see another flat mockup floating on a gradient. They'll see:

1. A phone on a real workbench with a real wrench next to it — and the screen is *glowing* with a clear "oil change overdue" message. That's a story in one second.
2. As they scroll through the screenshots, the real-world environments build trust and quality perception. This app was made by someone who cares.
3. The monospace captions and zero-radius aesthetic carry through every shot, reinforcing brand consistency.
4. By the sixth screenshot (the theme gallery), they're not just considering the app — they're considering which theme they'd pick. That's desire, not just interest.

The competitors are all showing the same thing: flat device mockups on solid or gradient backgrounds. You won't look like any of them.
